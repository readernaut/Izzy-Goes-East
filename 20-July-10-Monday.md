# PART FOUR: GOING HOME (WHEREVER THAT IS)

---

## July 10th, Monday
### Manila (Again), 8:00 PM

We're back at Lola's house.

It feels different this time. Not overwhelming. Familiar.

When we walked in, Lola was at the door. She grabbed me first (not Mum, ME) and hugged me so tight I couldn't breathe.

"Apo ko," she said. "You came back."

"We said we would."

"Sometimes people say and don't do. You did."

Maya was there too. She's taller, which is impossible because it's been like a week and a half, but I swear she's taller.

"You're not taller," she said when I told her.

"I think you are."

"Maybe you shrunk."

We caught up over dinner (rice, obviously, and kare-kare this time, which is this peanut sauce stew thing that is INCREDIBLE). I told her about Japan and Taiwan. She showed me photos from what she's been doing (mostly school stuff, she's on break but there's summer enrichment or something).

"What was your favorite part?" she asked.

I thought about it.

"I don't know. Different parts of each place. In Japan, the gates at Fushimi Inari. In Taiwan, the night market. And here..." I looked around the table. Lola, the titas and titos, all the cousins, Mum and Dad and Sofia, Maya next to me. "Here, I think this. Just... this."

She nodded like she understood.

---

### Lola's Porch, 11:00 PM (Last Night)

I sat with Lola again.

Last time.

"I'm sad to leave," I said.

"But you'll come back."

"I hope so. School, though. And Mum's work. It's hard to..."

"I know." She patted my hand. "Life is full. But you will find a way. I know you will."

We sat there for a while, listening to the karaoke down the street, the dogs barking, the sounds of the neighborhood.

"Lola?"

"Yes, apo?"

"Am I Filipino?"

She looked at me. Really looked.

"You are my grandchild," she said. "You are Mari's daughter. You have our blood, our family, our stories in you. Wherever you go, whatever you do, that doesn't change. Filipino is not a passport. It's a connection. And we are connected, yes?"

"Yes."

"Then you are Filipino. Your kind of Filipino. Different from mine, different from Maya's, different from your mother's. But yours."

I cried.

She held my hand and didn't say anything else.

Sometimes that's enough.
