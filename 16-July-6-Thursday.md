## July 6th, Thursday
### Taipei, Day One

Taiwan is like... what if a city was really good at being a city?

Everything works. The metro is clean and cheap and goes everywhere. The convenience stores are like Japan's but with different snacks. The streets are busy but navigable.

And the FOOD. There are food stalls everywhere. Just little carts selling one thing each, and people lining up for them, and everything smells incredible.

Auntie Grace took us around the neighborhood. She's lived here for five years but grew up in Los Angeles, so she's experiencing Taiwan as an adult who chose to return, not someone who never left.

"It's different for me," she said when I asked. "I'm learning my heritage too. Not born here, but my parents are from here, and now I'm here. It's complicated."

Complicated seems to be the theme of this trip.

---

### Chiang Kai-shek Memorial Hall, 2:00 PM

We went to this huge memorial for a historical leader.

The building is massive, all white and blue, with the longest stairs I've ever climbed.

There's a guard-changing ceremony that happens every hour, very formal, very choreographed.

Mum tried to explain the history but it's complicated. Something about the Chinese Civil War and the Nationalists coming to Taiwan and the relationship with China now and...

"You'll learn it eventually," she said when she saw my confused face. "For now just appreciate the architecture."

Fair enough.

---

### Night Market!!!!!!!, 8:00 PM

OKAY.

THE NIGHT MARKET.

IS.

INCREDIBLE.

Lily was not lying about being excited.

It's like a giant outdoor food festival every night. Hundreds of stalls. Every kind of food you can imagine. Games. Souvenirs. Clothes. Everything.

I tried:
- Stinky tofu (smells terrible, tastes... okay? Maybe an acquired taste)
- Bubble tea from an actual Taiwanese place (THE BEST)
- Something called "gua bao" again but different from Auntie Grace's version
- Fried chicken on a stick
- Shaved ice with toppings

Sofia ate an entire sausage that was as big as her forearm.

Dad tried the stinky tofu and his face was PRICELESS.

Mum bought a bunch of snacks to bring back to London.

Lily led us around like she owned the place. She knew which stalls were best, which ones to skip, where to find the good bubble tea (apparently not all bubble tea is equal, there are tiers).

"You know a lot about this," I said.

"I come every week," she said. "It's the best part of living here."

---

### Walking Home, 10:00 PM

Lily and I walked behind the adults on the way home.

"Do you like living here?" I asked.

She shrugged. "It's okay. I miss LA sometimes. My friends there. But here is good too."

"Do you speak Chinese?"

"Mandarin. A little. Better than before. Mom makes me take lessons." She made a face. "The tones are hard."

"I'm trying to learn Tagalog," I said. "It's also hard."

"Languages are hard when you didn't grow up with them."

"Yeah."

We walked in silence for a bit.

"But you're trying," she said. "That's what matters. My mom says trying is the first step."

Auntie Grace wisdom, apparently.
