### June 24th, Saturday
### NINOY AQUINO INTERNATIONAL AIRPORT, MANILA, 7:48 AM (Manila Time, which is 12:48 AM London Time, which is WHY I FEEL LIKE A ZOMBIE)

WE LANDED.

My legs feel like they belong to someone else. My eyes are doing that thing where they're open but not really working. Tilly somehow looks fresh and energetic, like she didn't just spend thirteen hours in a metal tube. Dad is muttering about how long the immigration queue is. Mum is GLOWING.

Seriously. She looks ten years younger. She keeps saying things in Tagalog under her breath and smiling at nothing.

The airport is busy in a way that feels different from Heathrow. More chaotic, but not in a bad way? Like everyone knows where they're going but no one's in a hurry to pretend there's a system.

There are so many signs with Tagalog and English mixed together. I caught one that said "Welcome Balikbayans!" which I think means returning overseas Filipinos. I asked Mum if that means us and she got emotional.

We're in the queue for immigration now. The officer is stamping passports really fast and calling "next, next, next" without looking up.

Mum is holding my hand.

She hasn't held my hand in public in like two years.

I'm going to let her.

---

### Immigration Hall, 8:15 AM

The immigration officer looked at my passport, looked at me, looked at my passport again.

"British?"

"Yes."

"Filipina?"

I didn't know how to answer that. "My mum is," I said.

He nodded and stamped me through without another word.

Filipina?

Am I Filipina?

I mean, technically, maybe? Half? But I've never called myself that. I've always said "half-Filipino" or "part-Filipino" or just "British, but my mum's from the Philippines."

Filipina sounds like something you have to earn.

---

### Arrivals Hall, 8:34 AM

THEY'RE HERE.

When we walked out of the arrivals gate, there was a CROWD. Like, a whole crowd of people with signs and flowers and balloons.

And one of those groups was for US.

Lola was at the front, tiny and grey-haired and crying already. Behind her was Tita Nene (I recognized her from photos), Tita Gemma, Tito Boy, and what looked like approximately seventeen cousins of varying sizes.

They were holding a sign that said "WELCOME HOME SANTOS-CRAWFORD FAMILY" with little Philippine and UK flags drawn on it.

Mum made this sound I've never heard her make before. Like a gasp and a sob combined. And then she just... ran to them. Actually ran. Left her suitcase and ran.

Lola grabbed her and they stood there crying and talking in Tagalog so fast I couldn't catch anything. The aunts and uncles surrounded them, everyone touching everyone, hands on arms and shoulders and faces.

Dad stood behind me with the suitcases, looking a bit lost but happy. He's used to this, I think. He knows he's the outsider here and he's okay with it.

Tilly ran in too. She's young enough that she can just hug strangers because they're family and it's not weird.

And I just... stood there.

Because I didn't know what to do.

And then Lola looked up, right at me, and she said something to Mum, and Mum pointed at me and said "oo, Izzy, si Izzy," and Lola's whole face changed.

She walked towards me, slowly, and I noticed she was using a cane now (Mum hadn't mentioned that), and she took my face in her hands, both hands, soft and warm, and she said:

"Apo ko. My grandchild. So big now. So beautiful."

And I didn't know what to say so I said "hi Lola" which is probably the most British response possible.

She laughed and hugged me anyway.

She smells like cooking. Like garlic and something sweet I don't recognize.

I'm writing this in the car (well, it's more like a van, they call it an "FX" apparently) and we're stuck in traffic on the way to Lola's house. Everyone is talking at once. Tilly is sitting on Tita Nene's lap even though she's too big for that. Mum is in the front seat talking to Tito Boy who's driving.

I'm in the back with two cousins who I think are called... JR? And... Jessa?

They smiled at me when I sat down.

"First time in Philippines?" the girl (Jessa? I think?) asked.

"Since I was four."

"Ay, so basically first time."

"Yeah. I don't remember anything."

She nodded. "Don't worry. We'll teach you."

I don't know what she meant by that exactly, but it didn't sound threatening.

It almost sounded... nice?

---

### Lola's House, Somewhere in Quezon City, 11:00 AM

Okay. A lot has happened.

First: the traffic. Holy moly, the TRAFFIC. It took us like two hours to go what seemed like not very far. There are jeepneys everywhere (these are the colorful bus-jeep things, basically the public transport here, and they have stuff written on them like "God Bless Our Trip" and "Kristine-Joy" and "Air-Conditioned: Wala" which means "none" so basically a sarcastic sign about how hot it is).

Second: the house.

When Mum described it, I imagined something small. And okay, it's not big, not by London standards. But it's not what I expected either. It's concrete, painted this faded pink, with a gate in front covered in plants. The windows have these metal grills on them, which Mum said is normal here, and there are two floors.

What I DIDN'T expect was how many people would be IN the house.

There's Lola.
Tita Nene, who lives here with her husband Tito Jun and their three kids: JP (17), Jessa (my age! 11!), and Marco (9).
Tita Gemma and Tito Boy, who have their own house but are here for our visit with their two kids: Gabby (14) and Kyle (8).
Plus a helper (Mum said that's what they call housekeepers here) named Ate Rosalie who's been with Lola for years.

So that's... eleven people who are USUALLY here or here right now. Plus us four. Fifteen people.

THE HOUSE IS NOT THAT BIG.

Mum said the house is "always open" and that's just how it is, there's always family around, people staying for meals or weeks or years. She said it like it was obvious, like anyone would understand. Dad looked slightly alarmed.

Third: the food.

We walked in and there was already food. Like, a LOT of food. On the table, on the counter, everywhere.

Lola immediately started saying "kain na, kain na" which means "eat now" and Mum had warned me this would happen, but I didn't realize it would be so... intense.

I wasn't even hungry (it was basically 4 AM London time) but Tita Nene put a plate in front of me with rice and chicken adobo and something that looked like vegetables in coconut milk and said "eat, eat, you're so thin!"

Reader, I am not thin. I am a perfectly normal eleven-year-old. But apparently here, I am THIN.

Everyone watched me eat.

Like, actually watched me.

Mum was eating too, making these happy noises, saying "Mum, your adobo, I missed it SO MUCH."

And Lola was smiling and also crying and also scolding Mum for not visiting sooner and also putting more rice on my plate.

There are a lot of emotions happening at once in this family.

Fourth: the heat.

It is SO HOT.

Like, I knew it would be hot. Mum said it would be hot. Everyone says the Philippines is hot. But this is a different kind of hot. It's hot AND humid, which means you're just constantly... damp. There's a fan in every room, sometimes two, and they're all going full blast, but they're just blowing hot air around.

Lola's house doesn't have air conditioning except in one room, which is Lola's room, and Mum said that's where we'll sleep, the four of us, on the floor on mats because Lola insists on giving us the bed but we insist on not taking it so we compromise by all being in the same room like sardines.

Very glamorous. Very "connect with your heritage."

Fifth: the cousins.

Jessa is cool. I think. She's shy but she showed me around the house and explained where everything is and when I asked where the bathroom was she walked me there and showed me how to use the tabo (it's a dipper, for... you know what, I'm not writing that down, but basically it's complicated) and she didn't make fun of me for not knowing.

JP is seventeen and mostly on his phone, which, fair.

Marco and Kyle are playing some game in the corner and haven't really acknowledged our existence.

Gabby is talking to Tilly and seems interested in her accent, keeps asking her to say things and then giggling.

I feel like everyone is being nice but also... watching me? To see what kind of cousin I am? If I'm going to be cool or weird or one of them or a foreigner?

I'm going to try to take a nap now. Mum said we need to sleep to adjust to the time zone.

It's very loud here. Lots of roosters (yes, roosters, in the middle of the city, apparently that's normal). Lots of traffic. Lots of people calling to each other.

But also kind of... nice?

I don't know.

I'm really tired.
