## June 24th, Saturday (still)
### Lola's House, 5:47 PM

I slept for like four hours and woke up completely confused about where I was.

For a second I thought I was in my room in Ealing and then I saw the fan on the ceiling and the unfamiliar walls and Tilly asleep next to me with her mouth open and I remembered: oh right, I'm on the other side of the world.

Mum is downstairs with Lola and the aunts. I can hear them talking and laughing. She sounds so DIFFERENT when she speaks Tagalog. Faster, louder, more animated. Like a different version of herself.

Dad is reading a book on the porch. He gave me a thumbs up when I came down.

Jessa invited me to watch TV with her and Marco. They were watching this show called "Wansapanataym" which is apparently a Filipino fantasy show and I couldn't understand any of it but Jessa kept translating the important parts.

"She's a fairy. He doesn't know. Now the villain is coming. He's her father! Plot twist!"

I appreciated the effort.

At one point, Tita Gemma came in and looked at me for a long moment and said, "You really look like Mari when she was young. Same eyes."

And something about that made me feel... seen? In a good way?

Like maybe I'm not as invisible here as I thought.

### Dinner, 8:00 PM

Filipino dinner is a whole THING.

First of all, everyone eats at the same time. Like, everyone drops what they're doing and comes to the table. No one eats separately in their room or in front of the TV (I learned this when I tried to take my plate to watch TV with Jessa and Tita Nene looked at me like I'd committed a crime).

Second, there's always rice. ALWAYS. Mum warned me about this. "Filipinos don't feel like they've eaten a meal if there's no rice." She was not exaggerating. There's a rice cooker the size of a small child and it's apparently on all day, producing fresh rice for every meal.

Third, everyone shares everything. The food goes in the middle of the table and you just... take from it. There are no individual portions. If you want more, you take more. If Lola thinks you haven't taken enough, she puts more on your plate without asking.

Fourth, there are comments. About everything.

"Izzy, eat more, you're so thin."
"Ay, you don't eat the okra? So picky!"
"She's like her mother, mestiza, but eat-eat, you need more curves."
"Beautiful skin, like her father, but too pale, we need to get some sun on you."

It's... a lot.

Mum shot me a look that said "I know, I know, just go with it."

But also, weirdly, it didn't feel mean? Like, in England if someone commented on your body like that you'd be mortified and they'd be rude. But here everyone's just... saying things. Observing. Like the weather.

I don't know if that makes it okay or not.

### After Dinner, 10:15 PM

Lola asked me to sit with her on the porch after dinner.

This was terrifying.

I don't really know Lola. I know she's my grandmother. I know she's important to Mum. I know she raised Mum and Mum's siblings and she's been widowed for ten years (Lolo died when I was little) and she's the center of the family.

But I don't KNOW her know her.

We sat on these plastic chairs (there are plastic chairs everywhere here, red and white, they seem to be the Official Chair of the Philippines) and she didn't say anything for a while. Just sat there with her fan, fanning herself slowly.

Then she said: "Your mother talks about you all the time."

"She does?"

"Izzy did this at school. Izzy is so smart. Izzy is reading this book. Izzy said this funny thing." She smiled. "I feel like I know you already. But now you're here."

"Yeah." I didn't know what else to say.

"You know what balikbayan means?"

"Like... returning Filipino?"

"Close. It means 'returning to country.' Balik is return, bayan is country, town, nation. When Filipinos go abroad and come back, they are balikbayan. Your mother is balikbayan. You..." She paused. "Maybe you are discovering the bayan for the first time. That's different. That's special too."

"I don't really feel special," I admitted. "I feel like I don't know anything."

She reached over and patted my hand. Her hand is so small. Papery skin, warm.

"Nobody expects you to know," she said. "You're not here to prove anything, apo. You're here to learn. To see. To meet your family." She smiled. "And to eat. Definitely to eat. You're too thin."

I laughed. I couldn't help it.

"There," she said. "That's better. Now, tell me about your school."

And we talked. For like an hour. She asked about my classes, my friends, what I like to do. She told me about how Mum was at my age, obsessed with drawing and always borrowing books from the library, and how she knew Mum would leave someday because "she was always looking at somewhere else."

"But now she's back," Lola said. "And she brought you. That's what matters."

When I came inside, Mum was watching from the doorway.

"Good talk?" she asked.

"Yeah, actually."

She hugged me. Which was weird because she doesn't do that very often anymore. I'm eleven, I'm too old for spontaneous hugs, supposedly.

But it was nice.

### Midnight, Lola's Room

Can't sleep.

Partly because Tilly is snoring. Partly because it's still really hot even with the AC on (the AC is ancient and sounds like a dying motorcycle). Partly because my brain won't turn off.

Today was a lot.

I keep thinking about what Lola said. About balikbayan versus discovering for the first time. Like there's a word for returning, but what's the word for arriving for the first time? For coming to a place that's supposed to be yours but doesn't feel like it yet?

Jessa lent me a book before bed. It's a Filipino young adult novel about a girl who can see ghosts. "It's really good," she said. "Filipino author. Let me know what you think."

I don't know if she was testing me or just being nice.

Both, maybe.

I'm going to try to read a bit and then sleep.

Tomorrow we're apparently going to a mall. Mum says malls here are different. She says you'll understand when you see it.

Everyone keeps saying stuff like that. "You'll understand when you see it."

I hope they're right.
