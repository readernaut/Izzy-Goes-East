## July 11th, Tuesday
### Airplane, Manila to London, Hour 2 of 13

We're going home.

I feel different.

Not transformed, like in movies, where the main character has some big realization and becomes a new person.

Just... different. Shifted. Like something moved inside me and settled in a new position.

I know things now I didn't know before.

I know what sinigang tastes like.
I know how to say mahal kita.
I know what it feels like to walk through a thousand red gates.
I know what the night market smells like.
I know I have a cousin named Jessa who likes ghost stories.
I know I have a grandmother who stayed up late to talk to me.
I know I belong to more places than I realized.

Maybe that's what this trip was for.

Not to make me Filipino (or make me more Filipino, or prove I'm Filipino).

Just to show me what's there. What I'm part of. What's been waiting for me all along.

---

### Hour 6 of 13

Tilly is asleep again. Girl could sleep through anything.

Dad is on his fourth movie. He's worked through most of the action section.

Mum is reading, but she keeps looking out the window.

"What are you thinking about?" I asked.

"Everything. Nothing. The trip."

"Did it go how you wanted?"

She smiled. "Better. You actually liked it."

"Why wouldn't I?"

"I worried you'd find it boring. Or too hot. Or too overwhelming. I worried you'd feel out of place and that would be my fault for not teaching you enough, for not taking you sooner."

"It was overwhelming," I said. "And hot. And sometimes I felt out of place. But I'm glad we went. Really glad."

She reached over and squeezed my hand.

"Me too, anak."

---

### Hour 11 of 13

Almost there.

I can't believe it's almost over.

But also, it's not over. Not really.

I'm going to text Jessa when we land. Keep up the Tagalog lessons. Watch that Filipino ghost show she told me about. Maybe convince Mum to send balikbayan boxes and add my own stuff in them.

I'm going to tell Roshni everything. Show her the photos. Make her try Filipino food (there's a place in Earl's Court, apparently).

I'm going to practice saying "Filipina" like it's something I'm allowed to say.

Because maybe I am.

Maybe Lola was right. Maybe Filipino is a connection, not a passport.

And I'm connected.
