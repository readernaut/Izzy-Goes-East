## June 26th, Monday
### Morning, Lola's House

Today's plan: visiting Intramuros.

Jessa explained it's the old walled city, from when the Spanish were here. Mum got very excited about showing us "real history" and Dad pulled out a guidebook he'd apparently been hiding, so I guess we're doing the tourist thing today.

But first: breakfast.

Filipino breakfast is insane. Like, in England, breakfast is cereal or toast or maybe eggs if someone's feeling fancy. Here, breakfast is a full MEAL. Rice (obviously). Some kind of meat, usually. Something called longganisa, which is a sweet sausage. Eggs. And coffee that's so strong I took one sip and felt my heartbeat in my eyeballs.

"You don't like the coffee?" Lola asked, noticing my face.

"It's very... intense."

She smiled. "Barako. Mountain coffee. Makes you strong."

"I think it might make me explode."

JP, who was walking through the kitchen, laughed. First time I've heard him make any sound directed at me. Progress.

---

### Intramuros, 11:30 AM

We took two jeepneys to get here. JEEPNEYS.

Okay, I need to describe this because nothing I've ever experienced compares.

A jeepney is basically a bus, but it's shaped like a stretched-out jeep with benches in the back. You get on by telling the driver where you're going and paying (like, actually handing money to other passengers who pass it forward to the driver, which is the most trusting system I've ever seen). Then you squeeze in with approximately twelve other people in a space designed for maybe eight.

There's no AC. Just open sides and a lot of wind.

Mum was grinning the whole time like this was the best thing ever. "I used to take these to school every day," she said.

Tilly was terrified at first but by the end she was laughing and saying she wanted to ride them everywhere.

Dad held onto his bag like it was going to escape.

I was somewhere in between. Terrified AND delighted.

Intramuros itself is really cool. Old stone walls, cobblestone streets, churches that are like 400 years old. Mum kept telling us things about the Spanish colonial period and the American period and World War II (apparently a lot of this was destroyed during the war and rebuilt).

We went to this church called San Agustin and Mum got quiet. "My parents got married here," she said softly.

I didn't know that.

There's so much I don't know.

---

### Fort Santiago, 2:00 PM

We went to Fort Santiago, which is this old Spanish fortress, and there's a museum there about José Rizal.

José Rizal, I learned today, is basically the Filipino national hero. He was a writer and a doctor and a really smart guy who wrote these novels criticizing Spanish rule and he was executed for it. His face is on money. There are Rizal streets and Rizal parks and Rizal statues EVERYWHERE.

In the museum there are these markers on the ground showing his last steps, from his cell to the execution site. We followed them. Mum was very quiet.

"When I was your age," she said to me and Tilly, "we had to memorize his poems for school. His last poem is called 'Mi Último Adiós.' My Last Farewell. He wrote it the night before he died."

"That's really sad," Tilly said.

"Yes. But it's also beautiful. He loved his country so much."

I thought about that. Loving a country so much you'd die for it.

I don't know if I love any country that much. I live in England but I'm not sure I'd die for it. The Philippines is part of me but I barely know it.

Maybe you have to live in a place, really live there, to love it that way.

Or maybe it's not about the place. Maybe it's about the people.

### Dinner with Tito Manny and His Family, 8:00 PM

Tonight Mum's oldest brother, Tito Manny, came for dinner with his wife and kids. He lives in a different part of the city (Makati, which is apparently the fancy business district) and he has a car (a BIG deal, according to Jessa, who whispered this to me like it was gossip).

Tito Manny is... a lot.

He's loud. Like, REALLY loud. He laughed at everything, slapped people on the back, made jokes I didn't understand, and immediately started teasing Dad about whether he could handle Filipino food.

"You like the spicy, James? Wait until you try labuyo, ha! It will make you cry!"

Dad smiled politely and said "I'm sure I'll manage" in his most British voice.

Tito Manny's kids are older: college-aged, a boy and a girl. They were nice but mostly talked to JP about university stuff. They speak English with American accents, which was weird because they've never lived in America. Jessa explained later that American English is what they learn in school, from movies, from everything.

"You're the weird ones," she said. "With your British accents."

"We're FROM Britain."

"I know. It's still weird."

At dinner, Tito Manny made everyone go around and say something they were grateful for. Very formal, very dramatic.

When it was my turn, I panicked and said "grateful for the food" which was basic but true.

Mum said she was grateful to be home.

Lola said she was grateful to have her children and grandchildren together.

Tilly said she was grateful for halo-halo, which made everyone laugh.
